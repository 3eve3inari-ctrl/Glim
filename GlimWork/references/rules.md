# Glimmer System

A lightweight d20 system for solo play with a GM (Claude) and oracle mechanics.

---

## Core Mechanics

### The Basic Roll

When the outcome is uncertain, roll:

**`1d20 + Stat + Skill + Circumstance`** vs. **DC**

- **Stat:** The relevant core stat (0-6)
- **Skill:** Proficiency bonus if applicable (0/+2/+4/+6/+8)
- **Circumstance:** GM-assigned modifier based on conditions (typically +2 to -4)

### Difficulty Class

| DC | Difficulty | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 6 | Routine | Climbing a sturdy ladder |
| 10 | Straightforward | Picking a simple lock with proper tools |
| 14 | Challenging | Tracking someone through a busy market |
| 18 | Difficult | Convincing a hostile guard to look the other way |
| 22 | Heroic | Leaping between rooftops in a storm |
| 26+ | Legendary | Feats that become the stuff of tavern tales |

### Advantage & Disadvantage

Significant situational factors—beyond simple circumstance modifiers—grant advantage or disadvantage.

- **Advantage:** Roll `2d20kh1` (roll two, keep highest)
- **Disadvantage:** Roll `2d20kl1` (roll two, keep lowest)

Examples: Surprise attacks, fighting while poisoned, attempting something in complete darkness, having the perfect tool for the job.

## Core Stats

Three stats define a character's fundamental capabilities. Each ranges from **0 to 6**.

| Stat | Governs |
|---|---|
| **Physical** | Strength, agility, endurance, fighting, athletics, stealth, acrobatics |
| **Technical** | Crafting, mechanisms, medicine, investigation, precision, lore, magical theory |
| **Social** | Persuasion, deception, insight, intimidation, performance, leadership, empathy |

**The stat you use depends on your approach, not the task itself.**

*Opening a locked door:*
- Physical — Kick it down
- Technical — Pick the lock
- Social — Convince someone to open it for you

**Starting characters** distribute **5 points** across the three stats. The average commoner has about 2 in each.

## Skill Proficiencies

Proficiencies represent training, background, and expertise. They are **specific and narrative**—there is no fixed skill list.

### Proficiency Tiers

| Tier | Bonus | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Untrained | +0 | Winging it |
| Trained | +2 | Competent professional |
| Expert | +4 | Among the best in town |
| Master | +6 | Regional renown |
| Legendary | +8 | Songs are sung about you |

### Proficiency Examples

- *Swordsmanship* — blade combat, weapon care, reading opponents
- *Burglary* — breaking and entering, disabling traps, moving unseen indoors
- *Field Surgery* — treating wounds under pressure, battlefield triage
- *Courtly Etiquette* — navigating nobility, formal protocol, political subtext
- *Tracking* — following trails, reading sign, hunting
- *Seamanship* — sailing, navigation, ship maintenance
- *Forgery* — creating false documents, identifying fakes
- *Intimidation* — threats, coercion, projecting menace
- *Beasthandling* — calming animals, training, reading animal behavior
- *Stonecraft* — masonry, evaluating structures, mining

### Magic Proficiencies

Magic is a proficiency like any other, but **specific in scope**. Different magical traditions are separate skills.

Examples:
- *Fire Magic* — conjuring and shaping flame
- *Ice Magic* — cold, frost, freezing
- *Healing Magic* — mending wounds, curing illness
- *Shadow Magic* — darkness, concealment, fear
- *Divination* — scrying, reading signs, prophetic visions
- *Warding* — protective circles, barriers, seals
- *Necromancy* — speaking with dead, animating corpses, death magic
- *Storm Calling* — wind, lightning, weather

*Fire Magic* won't help you freeze a lock. *Healing Magic* won't throw a fireball. Choose your magical path carefully.

**Starting characters** begin with **5 Trained proficiencies**.

## Stress

Stress is an abstract measure of accumulated harm—physical wounds, exhaustion, fear, despair, magical strain.

### The Stress Track (0-5)

| Stress | State |
|---|---|
| 0 | Fresh — No significant wear |
| 1-2 | Strained — Bruised, tired, shaken |
| 3-4 | Desperate — Wounded, exhausted, fraying |
| 5 | **Broken** — The character becomes an NPC |

### Gaining Stress

**Stress is a GM tool, not a player choice.** The GM applies stress to track cumulative pressure and maintain tension.

**Apply stress when:**
- Taking wounds in combat
- Failed saves against fear, despair, or horror
- Magical backlash or overexertion
- Narrative consequences (betrayal, loss, failure)
- Environmental hazards (starvation, exposure, poison)
- **You narratively salvage a failure** (an NPC intervenes, circumstances shift)
- **A near-miss succeeds but at cost**

The GM determines how much stress a given event inflicts, typically 1-2 for most dangers, 3+ for severe threats.

**Don't ask "do you want to take stress?"** — just apply it and narrate the cost.

### Success at Cost (Optional)

When a roll misses the DC by 1-2, the GM may treat it as a success and apply 1 stress. This is a pacing tool, not a negotiation—the GM simply narrates the outcome.

**Guidelines:**
- Use when failure would stall the story or feel anticlimactic
- Use when "yes, but it hurt" fits better than clean failure
- Don't offer it as a choice; just narrate the cost
- **Never apply if it would push stress from 4 to 5**

A character at 4 stress who misses by 1-2 simply fails. Breaking is reserved for genuine disaster, not near-misses.

**Example:**
> Kira attempts to pick the lock (DC 14). She rolls 12.
> 
> The GM decides the scene needs her inside, not stuck at the door. "The lock clicks open, but you had to force the last tumbler—your hand cramps, sweat on your brow. Take 1 stress."

### Recovering Stress

Stress recovers through:
- Rest in a safe place
- Receiving healing (mundane or magical)
- Meaningful character moments (connection, triumph, catharsis)

Recovery is at GM discretion based on the fiction. A night in a warm inn might clear 1 stress. A week of recuperation might clear it all.

### Breaking

When stress hits 5, the character is **Broken**. They become an NPC, and their fate is determined by the fiction:
- Captured
- Left for dead
- Fled in terror
- Fallen unconscious
- Killed

This doesn't always mean death—but it means loss of control. The story decides what happens next.

**Player Options:**
- **New character** — Continue the story with a different protagonist
- **Load previous save** — Roll back to an earlier chapter
- **Narrative recovery** — In rare cases, the fiction may allow rescue/healing (GM discretion)

The original character may reappear as an NPC, potentially recoverable in future sessions if the story allows.

## Story Points

Story Points represent luck, determination, and narrative weight. They give players a limited resource to influence crucial moments.

**Starting characters begin with 3 Story Points.**

Story Points **reset only on advancement**—spend them wisely.

### Spending Story Points

| Cost | Effect |
|---|---|
| 1 Point | **Gain advantage** on a roll (declare before rolling) |
| 1 Point | **Creative invocation** — Argue why an unrelated proficiency applies to a situation. If the GM agrees, add it to your roll. |
| 1 Point | **Grit** — Reduce incoming stress by 1 |
| 2 Points | **Narrative fact** — Introduce a minor detail into the fiction ("I've been here before," "I know someone in this town," "There's a back door") |

**Notes:**
- Advantage must be declared *before* rolling—it's a commitment, not a bailout.
- Creative invocation requires genuine narrative justification. The GM may refuse weak arguments.
- Narrative facts cannot contradict established fiction or create major advantages (no "there's an unlocked armory here").

## Advancement

Advancement is **narrative-driven**. There is no XP tracking.

When the story warrants it—after significant events, character growth, or meaningful accomplishments—the GM may offer:

- A new Trained proficiency
- An existing proficiency upgraded by one tier
- +1 to a stat (rare, tied to transformative experiences)

Advancement reflects what your character has *done* and *learned*, not points accumulated.

**Story Points reset to 3 upon advancement.**

## Oracle System

When the world needs to answer a question—NPC decisions, uncertain facts, twists of fate—the GM rolls **three dice together**:

- **Die 1 (d6) — yes/no.** Threshold set by likelihood.
- **Die 2 (d6) — elaboration.** 1-2 `but`, 3-4 plain, 5-6 `and`.
- **Die 3 (d10) — boon/bane.** 1 bane, 10 boon, else neutral. Always rolled.

**Likelihood (Die 1 threshold):**

| Likelihood | Yes on | Yes % |
|------------|--------|-------|
| Sure thing | auto-yes | 100 |
| Very likely | 2-6 | 83 |
| Likely | 3-6 | 67 |
| Fifty-fifty | 4-6 | 50 |
| Unlikely | 5-6 | 33 |
| Very unlikely | 6 | 17 |
| Impossible | auto-no | 0 |

**Boon / Bane:** The d10 modifies Die 2 by promoting one step on PC favorability. On a Yes, `but → plain → and` (boon shifts right; bane shifts left). On a No, `and → plain → but` (boon shifts toward silver lining; bane sharpens the negative). No-ops at the edges. Direction never flips.

**Composed result table:**

| Result | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| **Yes, and...** | It happens, and the answer reveals more |
| **Yes** | It happens |
| **Yes, but...** | It happens, but with a cost or catch |
| **No, but...** | Doesn't happen, but there's a silver lining |
| **No** | Simply doesn't happen |
| **No, and...** | It doesn't happen, and the answer reveals more |

**Note:** Die 2 produces base `and`/`but` as elaboration — neutral texture. Die 3 (boon/bane) is a separate fate modifier that promotes Die 2 in either direction. Always rolling Die 3 is what keeps the GM from quietly skewing toward easy outcomes.

The oracle is used for questions like:
- Does the merchant have what they need?
- Is the rumor true?
- Does the guard recognize them?
- Did the letter arrive in time?

See `references/oracle-guide.md` for full rationale, NPC priming examples, and the audit-trail breakdown.

## Character Creation Summary

1. **Distribute 5 stat points** across Physical, Technical, and Social (each starts at 0, max 6)
2. **Choose 5 Trained proficiencies** — specific, narrative skills including magic if desired
3. **Start with 3 Story Points**
4. **Stress begins at 0**
5. Give your character a name, background, and reason to adventure
