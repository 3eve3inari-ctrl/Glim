# The Glim

## Summary
The Glim is the term settlers use for the magical phenomenon that began appearing in the new lands roughly 25 years ago. It manifests as a faint shimmer in the air — a glimpse of light that catches the eye sideways, brighter at night, brightest near dungeon entrances. Where the Glim is, magic is possible. Beyond its reach, the world behaves the way the old books say it should: knife-and-fire, blood-and-bone, no spells.

## Key Facts
- **Onset:** First documented in the new lands ~25 years ago. No record of it in the homeland.
- **Appearance:** Faint silver-blue shimmer in the air. Visible to anyone with normal sight; clearer at night, around still water, and in the lee of high places.
- **Density:** Strongest near dungeons (places where the air feels "thick" with it) and weakens with distance. Most settler camps build in low-Glim zones for safety; Lantern Hold is an exception, sited in unusually bright Glim.
- **Use:** Settlers have learned to *siphon* the Glim — pulling it through reagents (silvered powder, charged copper, certain plant ashes) or through focused attention. Effects are inconsistent, often dangerous, sometimes spectacular.
- **No school:** There is no Glim academy in the homeland. Every settler practitioner is self-taught. Most die young or change in ways their friends find unsettling. The expedition's naturalist, Kenric Maul, is the closest thing to a scholar of it on the western shore.
- **The Vahn don't call it the Glim.** They have other names, depending on line and dialect. They also disagree among themselves about whether it is new at all, or whether it has merely *returned*.

## Relevance to Play
- **Magic requires the Glim.** No spell, charm, or enchantment works in places where the shimmer cannot be seen. PCs attempting magical effects in low-Glim regions face higher DCs or simple impossibility.
- **The Glim attracts monsters.** Bright-Glim zones see more dungeon mouths, more strange creatures, and more "wakings" — events where something steps out of a place no one can find later.
- **Glim use changes the user.** Practitioners often develop quirks: light sensitivity, dreams that cross over, an inability to lie under a clear sky. These should emerge in play, not be picked up front.
- **Likelihood weighting:** Most magic-related questions in low-Glim areas should be set to *unlikely* or *very unlikely*. Same questions in high-Glim areas can default to *fifty-fifty* or higher.
