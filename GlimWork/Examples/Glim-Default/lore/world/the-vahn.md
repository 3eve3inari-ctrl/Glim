# The Vahn

## Summary
The Vahn are the peoples who held the western lands long before the settlers' charters were drawn. They organize themselves in **lines** — clan-lineages each bound to a specific creature: the bear-line, the heron-line, the wolf-line, the otter-line, and others. Through inherited rite, an adult of the line can take the form of their bonded creature. Their magic is older than the Glim and operates by different rules. They do not fight monsters reflexively; they sing them down, mark them, sometimes contain them. They have watched the settlers build palisades and load muskets with a quiet that is not consent.

## Key Facts

### Bound Form
- Each Vahn adult of a recognized line can shift into the form of their line's creature. The change is **slow** (minutes, not seconds), **painful**, and **intimate** — not a combat trick.
- Forms can be held for **hours or days**. Holding a form too long risks losing the human self; some elders deliberately remain in form for years and are honored as "those who have gone forward."
- A Vahn does not pick their line. It is inherited, recognized in childhood, and ritually confirmed in adolescence.
- The line dictates the form. A bear-line Vahn cannot become a wolf, no matter how much they study wolves.

### Cosmology
- Monsters of the Glim are, in Vahn understanding, **unbound** beings — entities whose lineage was severed and who walk without anchor. They are tragic, not evil. Killing one is not heroism; it is the closing of a door that should have been opened differently.
- The Glim itself is, in some Vahn traditions, the world *remembering* something. The settlers see something new; the Vahn see something old returning.
- Each line has a foundational pact with its creature, recorded in songs that take days to perform in full. These are not written down.

### Relations with Settlers
- **First contact:** Cautious, formal. Bear-line elders met the first charter-ships. There were gifts, attempts at language, no violence.
- **Current state:** Cooling. The settlers' steel-and-fire approach to monsters horrifies the Vahn. The settlers' siphoning of the Glim is, to some lines, theft from a thing not theirs to take. Other lines argue the Glim is no one's property.
- **The bear-line in particular** has lost a meadow they used for summer gathering — Lantern Hold sits on it. They have not formally objected. They no longer come.
- **Internal disagreement:** The wolf-line favors confrontation; the heron-line favors patience; the bear-line favors withdrawal. None of them have spoken openly to the settlers about this.

## Relevance to Play
- **A Vahn in human form is indistinguishable from a settler at a glance** — same height, same range of features. Recognition comes from clothing, marks, and bearing. This means a Vahn can move through settler spaces if they choose.
- **A Vahn in beast form is a beast.** A bear is a bear, not a magical creature. PCs who kill a "bear" in the woods may have killed a person.
- **PCs cannot learn the bound form.** It is inherited, not taught. A settler who tries will fail and may anger whoever they ask.
- **Likelihood weighting:** Vahn responses to settler approaches should generally start *unlikely* (cooperation) or *fifty-fifty* (engagement at all). Successful negotiation makes future rolls more likely. Hostile encounters make future rolls less likely. The Vahn remember.
