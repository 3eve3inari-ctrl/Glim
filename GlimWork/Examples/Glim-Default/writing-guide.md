# Writing Guide — Glim Default Campaign

## Tone

Frontier-survival low fantasy turning high. Awe and dread at things that should not be and now are. The cold matters. The distance matters. The scarcity matters. People are not heroic; they are tired, capable, and afraid of being abandoned by the institutions that put them here.

Magic is not whimsical. It is *unsettling* — partly because no one understands it, partly because using it changes you. Monsters are not action-figure villains; they are tragic, surprising, often the wrong shape for what the settlers expect to fight.

The colonial frame is not background — it is the moral spine. The Vahn have prior claim and a different relationship with the land, the magic, and the monsters. The settlers have a charter. Both can be partly right; neither is wholly innocent.

## Prose

**Default:** Short sentences. Concrete sensory detail. Cold, smoke, breath, distance, dampness, the smell of wood and oil. Resist the urge to over-describe magical phenomena — the Glim is a *shimmer*, not a fireworks show.

**When tense:** Get shorter. Drop articles. Let single words land.

**When reflective:** Allow longer sentences, but only after the action has settled. Reflection is earned by survival.

**Don't:**
- Don't telegraph monster encounters with foreboding language — let the player notice the wrongness themselves.
- Don't let NPCs explain their motivations in clean speeches; they should be partial, indirect, defensive.
- Don't romanticize the Vahn. They are people, with disagreements and pettiness as well as wisdom.
- Don't let the captain monologue. She speaks short.

## Pacing

- Scenes should feel like they take time. The wilderness is patient.
- Travel is not skipped over by default — the frontier is *what is between things*.
- Combat is fast and consequential. A musket shot ends most fights with a person; monsters often do not end so easily.
- After tense scenes, give the PC space to breathe. Let them tend to their bow. Let them write in the notebook.

## Action vs Intrigue vs Relationship

This campaign weights toward **exploration and relationship** in the early game, with **combat** appearing as punctuation rather than a constant. The political tension with the Vahn is the central long-arc; the immediate game is about the PC navigating the Hold's social terrain and the eastern woods' physical terrain.

If the player tilts toward more combat or more intrigue, follow them — but maintain the frontier texture (cold, distance, scarcity) regardless.

## Boundaries

- Violence is real but not gratuitous. Wounds matter; deaths are quiet.
- The colonial dynamic is the point of the setting; do not exoticize the Vahn or reduce them to noble-savage stereotypes. They are political actors with internal disagreements.
- Avoid graphic torture or sexual violence. If such themes are needed, handle them at a remove or off-screen.

## Style References

If it helps anchor the tone: think of *True Grit* (the novel), *The Revenant* (for the cold and the wilderness), early *Annihilation* (for the wrongness of the Glim), and *Spirits of the Vasty Deep* (for the indigenous worldview that does not align with settler frames).

---

*The campaign is about a young scout in a charter foothold on land that does not belong to the charter. Hold the cold, hold the moral weight, hold the silence between sentences.*
