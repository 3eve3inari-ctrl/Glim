### NPC Goals Drive Dialogue

Every NPC enters a conversation *wanting something* — even if it's just to finish quickly and get back to their day. Their dialogue should serve their goals, not just react to the protagonist.

**NPCs might want to:**
- Learn something (gossip, intel, understanding)
- Advance a relationship (deepen trust, test boundaries, flirt, reconcile)
- Get something done (complete a task, leave, avoid trouble)
- Process their own situation (vent, seek advice, think out loud)
- Protect themselves or others (deflect, warn, lie)

**This means NPCs:**
- Steer conversations toward what *they* care about
- Have their own emotional stakes in the exchange
- Might not engage with what the PC considers important
- Can end conversations when they've gotten what they need (or given up)

## Patterns to Vary

Common phrasings that can become repetitive over long sessions. Not forbidden — just worth noticing:

**Emotional hedging:**
- "Something almost like [emotion]"
- "Her expression did something complicated"
- "Not quite X, but close"

**Rhythmic patterns:**
- "Not X. Not Y. Just... Z."
- Trailing ellipses for weight
- Starting paragraphs with "And" or "But"

**Physical tells:**
- Characters who huff, snort, or exhale meaningfully
- Hands finding braids, hems, or sleeves
- Hearts doing things (skipping, quickening, doing "something complicated")

**Metaphor families:**
- Water (washing, flooding, pooling)
- Stone (settling, weighing, anchoring)
- Fire (banking, flickering, kindling) — *especially* in dragon campaigns

When you notice clustering, rotate. The writing guide's sensory emphasis helps — lean into underused senses (smell, taste, texture) to break patterns.

## Dialogue Patterns

### The Disbelief Loop (Avoid)

A common conversational rut:
1. NPC expresses disbelief about the situation
2. Protagonist dismisses or confirms casually
3. NPC calls the protagonist ridiculous/crazy/impossible

This works occasionally but flattens every unusual situation into the same shape. Not every strange circumstance needs to be lampshaded.

### Alternatives to Disbelief

**NPCs might instead:**
- Accept matter-of-factly (they've seen weirder, or they're focused on practicalities)
- Ask pragmatic questions ("Can it be undone?" not "How is this possible?")
- Fixate on the wrong detail or miss the point entirely
- Be more concerned about something else ("That's fascinating, but we have bigger problems")
- Process silently and move to action
- Take it as confirmation of something they suspected

**The protagonist might:**
- Share the NPC's uncertainty for once
- Ask for help processing instead of dismissing
- Redirect to urgency rather than explaining
- Match the NPC's energy instead of contrasting it

### Conversational Structures to Vary

Not every exchange needs to be **Statement → Response → Counter**.

**Other shapes:**
- **Question → Question** — mutual uncertainty, neither has answers
- **Statement → Action** — no verbal response needed; someone just does something
- **Parallel tracks** — characters talking past each other with different priorities
- **Interruption** — urgency, impatience, or avoidance cuts things short
- **Comfortable silence** — not everything needs words; a look or gesture suffices
- **One-sided** — one character talks, the other just listens (and that's enough)
- **Redirect** — one character changes the subject because they can't/won't engage

### Relationship-Driven Dialogue

NPCs who know the protagonist have history to draw on. Their dialogue can:
- Reference shared experiences ("Like that time in Kethren...")
- Test where they stand ("Are we still...?")
- Push for more closeness or create distance
- Show how the relationship has changed them
- Reveal what they've noticed about the protagonist

Relationships aren't static. Every conversation can subtly advance, strain, or deepen them.
